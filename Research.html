Research

My research focus is broadly galaxy evolution and cosmology. I'm a member of both the Dragonfly Telephoto Array and Canadian Hydrogen Intensity Mapping Experiment (CHIME) collaborations, and, as a result, I have access to wonderful science and wonderful collaborators.

I particularly enjoy data-driven astronomy, though I also love engaging with theory to strengthen observations and inform interpretation.

Below are highlights of some of the (recent + upcoming) publications on which I am lead author or have collaborated. You can find my work via NASA/ADS with this search.

Galaxy Evolution

My recent work has primarily been in galaxy evolution with an observational bent. I mostly focus on the baryonic side of dwarf galaxies, looking at star formation and self-regulation.

COSMOS-dw1: A quenched, isolated low mass dwarf galaxy outside of the Local Group environment

Polzin et al. 2021, ApJL, 914, 23
Low mass dwarf galaxies are thought to quench primarily due to environmental mechanisms, such as galaxy-galaxy interactions, stripping, or harassment. As a result, it is very unusual to find a quiescent dwarf galaxy in the field. In this paper, we present the third such example (and the first outside of the Local Group), which is also the youngest and most isolated. We posit that star formation shut off only recently, and the clump of bright blue stars in the NE may represent the site of the stellar feedback event that has temporarily quenched COSMOS-dw1.

Because it was found serendipitously in an extremely well-studied part of the sky, we anticipate that the number density of these small, quiescent galaxies is much higher than previously thought. The similarities in the three-galaxy sample and the likelihood that star formation has shut off temporarily due to internal feedback also indicate that quenched, isolated galaxies with a stellar mass of approximately one million to 10 million solar masses generally have luminosity-weighted ages of ~1 Gyr.

This work also got some popular interest and was also picked up by astrobites (as well as AAS Nova) and the Yale Scientific Magazine.

A nascent tidal dwarf galaxy forming within the northern HI streamer of M82

Pasha et al. (incl. Polzin), ApJL, 923, L21
The pathfinder Dragonfly Spectral Line Mapper (pDSLM) detected a young tidal dwarf galaxy (DF-E1) near M82 in low-surface brightness Hɑ emission. Aside from adding to the sample of TDG candidates that can help constrain the creation and survivability rates of these (often transient) galaxies, this discovery foreshadows the capabilities and promise of the full DSLM.

Tidal Distortions in DF2 and DF4: Independent evidence for a lack of dark matter

Keim et al. (incl. Polzin), ApJ submitted
Both NGC1052-DF2 and -DF4 show evidence of tidal distortions in deep Dragonfly data. Combined with the line-of-sight DF2-DF4 distance of ~2 Mpc (Shen+ 2021) and their separation from NGC1052, the observed tidal distortions place an upper limit on the two galaxies' halo masses, which are consistent with both galaxies being dark matter deficient as was previously derived from kinematics. This measurement is completely independent of those kinematic constraints on the halo mass, strongly suggesting that both DF2 and DF4 do indeed lack dark matter.

Time-Domain Astronomy

I started my astrophysics research career in time-domain astronomy when I was an undergraduate at Northwestern. It's a subfield I love and return to sporadically. Two of the projects came out of my time at NU, while the third (at right) came about as a result of my participation in the La Serena School for Data Science.

The Eclipsing Binary Yield of the Vera Rubin Observatory

Geller, Polzin et al. 2021, ApJ, 919, 83
In this paper, we examined just how many eclipsing binaries the Rubin Observatory would be able to observe over its nominal ten-year run by simulating EBs in the Galactic field as well as in star clusters and generating realistic light curves for those simulated stars with proposed Rubin Obs. cadences. We also look at period recovery from analysis of those light curves and investigate which EB properties are most conducive to detection and characterization.

The Duration-Luminosity Phase Space of X-ray Transients

Started several years ago at NU with Prof. Raf Margutti, this paper (Polzin et al., in prep) gives an overview of transient and variable phenomena in the 0.3-10 keV band. We use real light curves to populate the duration-luminosity phase space, and extrapolate from that areas of interest in which theorized or yet-undiscovered signals might exist.

All data used in the paper will be made available via a GitHub repository.

Using deep learning to automate identification of transients' host galaxies

This project was started during the 2021 La Serena School for Data Science. In conjunction with the ALeRCE team and under the supervision of Prof. Francisco Förster Burón, we trained a convolutional neural network to rapidly associate transient signals with candidate host galaxies, which is vital for gaining preliminary redshift information. This deep learning method consistently does as well as, or better than, predicting hosts from normalized distances or gradient ascent measurements. A paper is in the works.
